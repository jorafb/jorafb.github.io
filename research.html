Penetration of a convection zone into a stable-stratified fluid

Giant planets like Jupiter, which undergo strong convection in their gaseous envelopes, might develop composition gradients from their formation history. During the early evolution of a giant planet, a convection zone propagates inwards as the planet cools down. How quickly does the convection zone move inward? What is the mixing mechanism behind this process? Using numerical simulations, in Fuentes and Cumming (2020) we found that the rate at which the convective zone evolves depends on how quickly it cools down and how large is the initial composition gradient (a large composition gradient makes the evolution slower). Further, we found that turbulent motions near the boundary of the convective zone carry enough energy to lift and mix heavier fluid from below. This process is called "entrainment" and it plays an important role in the growth of the convective zone. We construct a simple analytical model that captures the numerical results.

Formation and suppression of shear flows in a growing convection zone cooled from above

Shear flows can form spontaneously in 2D simulations of convection. These flows disperse convective plumes and decrease the vertical transport, making it difficult to study convection with 2D simulations. What is the physical mechanism behind the formation of these flows? Can they be avoided in 2D? We investigated this in Fuentes and Cumming (2021) by using a series of simulations of penetrative convection. We found that small shear perturbations grow in amplitude by taking energy from the convective motions, leading to the formation of strong shear flows. However, these perturbations are supressed in domains of large aspect ratio.
